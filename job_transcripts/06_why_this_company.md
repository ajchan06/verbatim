INTERVIEWER: Why do you want to work at Great Question?

CANDIDATE: A few reasons, in order of how much they actually matter to me. The first is the problem space. Customer research interviews are a goldmine that companies don't mine — and the AI moment is the first time that's been technically tractable. Being early to that, with a team building the platform layer, is interesting in a way that a lot of AI startups aren't. Most AI startups are building the same chatbot. You're building the workflow that comes after the chatbot.

INTERVIEWER: What else?

CANDIDATE: The job post itself. The four example projects you listed — semantic search over interview hours, MCP tools, agentic moderators, evals — those are exactly the four things I most want to learn. So this isn't "AI internship that happens to be available." It's "the work I'm already trying to do, paid, with mentorship."

INTERVIEWER: How do you know that's the work you want to do?

CANDIDATE: Because I spent a weekend building a small version of three of those four projects on my own, before knowing if I'd get the role. If I get the role, I get to do it for real. If I don't, I still spent a weekend learning the agent and MCP stack, which is the right bet regardless.

INTERVIEWER: What concerns do you have about the role?

CANDIDATE: Honestly, that I'm an intern, which means three months. Three months is short for any meaningful project to ship. So my concern is over-promising on what I can deliver in that window. I'd rather scope something small and ship it solidly than try to build everything and ship nothing.

INTERVIEWER: Where do you see yourself in five years?

CANDIDATE: I don't have a clean answer to that. The honest answer is, doing AI engineering work that has real users. Whether that's at a startup, a research lab, or my own thing — depends on what I learn between now and then. I'm not trying to optimize for a title.

INTERVIEWER: One last question. If we hired you, what would you want to be told on day one?

CANDIDATE: That I'm allowed to ask dumb questions for the first two weeks. The biggest mistake interns make is pretending to understand things to look smart. The second biggest is being too cautious to ship. So I'd want to be told the bar for asking and the bar for shipping — what's the team's threshold for each.

INTERVIEWER: That's actually a great closing answer.
